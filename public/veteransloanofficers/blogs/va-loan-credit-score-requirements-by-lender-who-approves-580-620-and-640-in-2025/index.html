VA Loan Credit Score Requirements by Lender: Who Approves 580, 620, and 640+ in 2025

Complete breakdown of VA loan credit score requirements by lender type—which banks approve 580, who requires 640+, and how manual underwriting helps middle-credit veterans qualify.

The VA doesn’t set a minimum credit score for VA loans—eligibility depends on military service, not your FICO score. However, lenders impose their own credit score minimums, called overlays, and these vary dramatically. Some VA specialists approve 580 scores while major banks require 640 or higher, meaning your lender choice directly affects whether you qualify.

Understanding which lenders approve your credit profile—and how to strengthen your application if you’re borderline—can mean the difference between approval and denial, or between a competitive rate and an expensive one. This guide breaks down VA loan credit score requirements by lender type, explains how manual underwriting works for veterans with middle credit scores, and shows you which compensating factors can overcome lower scores.

VA Loan Credit Score Requirements: The Official Rule

The Department of Veterans Affairs guarantees VA loans but doesn’t originate them. Private lenders (banks, credit unions, mortgage companies) issue VA loans and use the VA’s guarantee to reduce their risk. Because of this structure:

The VA has no official minimum credit score requirement.

Instead, VA guidelines state that lenders must perform a “satisfactory credit analysis” and that borrowers with credit scores below 620 may require additional scrutiny through manual underwriting. This gives lenders discretion to set their own minimums, resulting in wide variation across the industry.

Credit Score Requirements by Lender Type

Big Banks (Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo): 640-660 Minimum

Major retail banks typically require 640-660 credit scores for VA loans. They impose strict overlays to minimize risk and rarely use manual underwriting to approve borderline applications.

Why Big Banks Have Higher Minimums:

  • Automated underwriting (DU/LP) rejects most scores below 640
  • Internal risk policies prioritize high-credit borrowers
  • Limited appetite for manual underwriting (time-consuming and labor-intensive)
  • Focus on high-volume, low-touch lending

Pros of Big Banks:

  • Competitive rates for high-credit borrowers (700+)
  • Branch access for in-person service
  • May offer relationship discounts (if you have checking/savings accounts)

Cons of Big Banks:

  • Won’t approve 580-639 credit scores, even with strong compensating factors
  • Rigid underwriting (little flexibility for unique situations)
  • Loan officers often lack military lending expertise (mishandle BAH, deployment pay, etc.)

Bottom Line: If your credit score is 660+, big banks are competitive. Below 660, look elsewhere.

Credit Unions (Navy Federal, PenFed, USAA Partners): 620-640 Minimum

Military-focused credit unions typically require 620-640 credit scores, with some flexibility for members with strong service records or compensating factors.

Why Credit Unions Are More Flexible:

  • Member-owned structure (prioritize service over profit)
  • Familiarity with military pay structures (BAH, disability compensation, deployment pay)
  • Willingness to use manual underwriting for members with solid compensating factors
  • Longstanding relationships with veteran members

Pros of Credit Unions:

  • Lower fees than big banks
  • Better customer service and military lending knowledge
  • More flexibility with 620-660 credit scores
  • May waive certain fees for members

Cons of Credit Unions:

  • Membership requirements (military service, family member, or specific employer/location)
  • Rates sometimes slightly higher than big banks for top-tier credit
  • Limited geographic reach (some require you to be in specific states)

Bottom Line: Credit unions are ideal for veterans with credit scores in the 620-660 range who need a lender familiar with military income and willing to work through compensating factors.

VA Specialist Lenders (Veterans United, Freedom Mortgage, Quicken Loans/Rocket): 580-620 Minimum

Lenders who specialize in VA loans often approve 580-620 credit scores through manual underwriting. They focus exclusively (or primarily) on military lending and have in-house teams trained to evaluate residual income, disability compensation, and other VA-specific factors.

Why VA Specialists Approve Lower Scores:

  • Manual underwriting teams evaluate full financial picture, not just credit score
  • Deep knowledge of VA residual income rules (often approve borrowers conventional lenders deny)
  • Experience with military pay structures (BAH, hazard pay, tax-exempt combat income)
  • Business model built around serving credit-challenged veterans

Pros of VA Specialists:

  • Approve 580-639 credit scores with compensating factors
  • Understand deployment scenarios, COE issues, funding fee exemptions
  • Online platforms designed for remote/deployed service members
  • Fast processing (many close in 30-40 days)

Cons of VA Specialists:

  • Rates may be slightly higher than big banks for excellent credit (700+)
  • Heavy reliance on online/phone communication (limited in-person options)
  • Origination fees sometimes higher than credit unions

Bottom Line: If your credit score is 580-639, VA specialist lenders are your best option. They approve scores big banks automatically deny and know how to leverage military income to support approval.

What Is Manual Underwriting and How Does It Help?

Manual underwriting means a human underwriter reviews your full financial profile instead of relying solely on automated systems (Desktop Underwriter or Loan Prospector). For veterans with middle credit scores (580-639), manual underwriting can approve loans that automated systems reject.

When Lenders Use Manual Underwriting

  • Credit score below 620 (VA guideline threshold)
  • Recent bankruptcy, foreclosure, or short sale (after required waiting periods)
  • High debt-to-income ratio (41%+)
  • Non-traditional credit history (thin credit file, recent credit repair)
  • Complex income situations (self-employment, deployment pay, disability compensation)

How Manual Underwriting Evaluates You

Instead of just looking at your credit score, manual underwriters assess:

1. Residual Income
The amount of money left each month after paying mortgage, debts, taxes, and estimated living expenses. VA residual income thresholds vary by region and family size. Strong residual income can offset a lower credit score.

Example: A veteran with a 600 credit score but $2,000/month in residual income (well above VA minimums) may be approved over a 640-score borrower with marginal residual income.

2. Payment History
Recent payment patterns matter more than old issues. If you have a 590 score due to collections from 5 years ago but 24 months of perfect mortgage/rent payments, manual underwriters view that favorably.

3. Compensating Factors
Strong positives that offset credit concerns:

  • Large cash reserves (6+ months of mortgage payments in savings)
  • Low debt-to-income ratio (under 35%)
  • Stable employment history (2+ years same employer)
  • VA disability compensation (counts as income, doesn’t count against DTI)
  • Down payment (even small amounts like 5-10% strengthen applications)

4. Explanation Letters
You can explain credit issues (medical debt, divorce, military deployment financial hardship) to provide context. Manual underwriters consider these explanations, whereas automated systems ignore them.

Is It Worth Waiting to Improve Your Score?

If you’re near a pricing threshold (680, 700, 720), waiting 2-4 months to boost your score can save tens of thousands of dollars. Strategies to quickly improve credit:

  • Pay down credit card balances to under 30% utilization (ideally under 10%)
  • Become an authorized user on a family member’s established credit card (inherit their positive payment history)
  • Dispute inaccurate items on your credit report (collections you paid, accounts that aren’t yours)
  • Avoid opening new credit accounts (hard inquiries drop scores temporarily)

However, if you’re at 590 and need housing immediately (expiring lease, family situation, PCS orders), waiting 6-12 months to reach 640 may not be practical. In that case, accept a higher rate now and refinance through VA IRRRL once your score improves.

How to Qualify with a 580-639 Credit Score

If your credit score is in the 580-639 range, here’s how to maximize approval odds:

1. Work with VA Specialist Lenders

Big banks auto-deny below 640. VA specialists evaluate your full profile through manual underwriting. Target lenders like:

  • Veterans United Home Loans
  • Freedom Mortgage
  • Quicken Loans/Rocket Mortgage (VA division)
  • Navy Federal Credit Union (members only)
  • PenFed Credit Union (members only)

Compare VA lenders at BrowseLenders.com to find those who approve your credit range.

2. Maximize Residual Income

VA residual income requirements are lower than conventional DTI limits, but exceeding minimums strengthens your application. Ways to boost residual income:

  • Include VA disability compensation: Fully qualifying income, doesn’t count against DTI
  • Document BAH accurately: Many lenders mishandle BAH—ensure it’s counted fully
  • Pay off small debts before applying: Eliminating $200/month in credit card payments frees $200 in residual income
  • Show cash reserves: 3-6 months of mortgage payments in savings proves financial stability

3. Provide Explanation Letters for Credit Issues

If your low score stems from specific events (medical emergency, divorce, deployment-related financial hardship), write a detailed explanation letter. Include:

  • What happened (medical bills, identity theft, etc.)
  • How you’ve resolved the issue (payment plan, dispute, etc.)
  • Why it won’t happen again (stable income, better budgeting, etc.)

Manual underwriters consider context that automated systems ignore.

4. Demonstrate 12-24 Months of Clean Payment History

Recent payment patterns outweigh old issues. If you’ve had no late payments in the past 12-24 months (even if your score is low due to old collections or charge-offs), emphasize this in your application.

5. Consider a Co-Borrower

Adding a spouse or family member with stronger credit can improve approval odds and rate pricing—but only if their income and credit strengthen the overall application. If their DTI is already high or credit is worse than yours, adding them may hurt more than help.

6. Offer a Small Down Payment

Even 5-10% down demonstrates commitment and reduces lender risk. While VA loans allow zero down, voluntarily putting money down can push a borderline application to approval.

Common Credit Score Mistakes Veterans Make

Mistake 1: Assuming the VA Sets a Minimum Score

Many veterans think the VA requires a specific credit score and give up when they see their 600 score. In reality, VA specialists approve 580+ scores routinely through manual underwriting.

Mistake 2: Only Applying to Big Banks

Applying exclusively to Chase, Bank of America, or Wells Fargo guarantees denial for scores below 640. Cast a wider net and target VA specialists who approve your range.

Mistake 3: Not Checking Credit Before Applying

Surprises on your credit report (collections you forgot about, identity theft, incorrect late payments) can derail applications. Pull your credit report from AnnualCreditReport.com before applying and dispute errors.

Mistake 4: Opening New Credit Right Before Applying

New credit cards, auto loans, or personal loans drop your score temporarily (hard inquiries) and increase your DTI. Avoid new credit for 6-12 months before applying for a mortgage.

Mistake 5: Ignoring Compensating Factors

Veterans with middle credit scores often assume they can’t qualify. In reality, strong residual income, disability compensation, or 12+ months of perfect rent payments can overcome a 600 score through manual underwriting.

Mistake 6: Not Shopping Multiple Lenders

One lender may deny your 620 score while another approves it. Always compare at least 3-5 VA lenders, especially if your credit is below 660.

How VA Disability Compensation Helps Approval

VA disability compensation strengthens your application in two powerful ways:

1. Counts as Qualifying Income
Disability payments are fully qualifying income for VA loans and don’t require tax returns or W-2s to verify—just your VA Benefits Summary Letter.

2. Doesn’t Count Against Debt-to-Income Ratio
While conventional lenders count all income against all debts, VA loans treat disability compensation more favorably. This effectively lowers your DTI and increases your buying power.

Example:

  • Monthly income: $5,000 base pay + $2,000 VA disability = $7,000 total
  • Monthly debts: $1,500 (car, student loans, proposed mortgage)
  • Conventional DTI: $1,500 ÷ $7,000 = 21.4%
  • VA effective DTI: Often calculated more favorably due to disability income treatment

Veterans with 10%+ disability ratings also skip the VA funding fee (saving $7,000-$16,000), making VA loans even more affordable and improving cash-to-close requirements.

Final Thoughts on VA Loan Credit Score Requirements

Your credit score matters for VA loans—but it’s not the only factor, and it doesn’t automatically disqualify you if it’s below 640. VA specialist lenders approve 580-620 scores daily through manual underwriting, especially for veterans with strong residual income, stable payment history, and military-related compensating factors.

If your credit score is lower than you’d like, focus on:

  • Working with VA lenders who approve your range
  • Maximizing residual income by documenting all military pay (BAH, disability compensation, deployment pay)
  • Building 12-24 months of clean payment history
  • Providing context for past credit issues through explanation letters
  • Shopping multiple lenders to find the best approval odds and rate

Your service earned you the VA loan benefit—don’t let a middle credit score prevent you from using it. The right lender, combined with strong compensating factors, can get you approved and into your home.
