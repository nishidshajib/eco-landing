Essential Knowledge for First-Time Homebuyers

Master the fundamentals before you start house hunting

Credit Score Preparation

Your credit score determines which loans you qualify for and what interest rate you will pay. Most first-time buyer programs require minimum scores between 580-620, but higher scores unlock better terms and lower costs over the loan life.

Down Payment Strategies

First-time buyers have more down payment options than they realize. From 3% conventional to 3.5% FHA to zero-down VA and USDA loans, understanding minimum requirements helps you buy sooner. Gift funds and assistance programs can bridge gaps in savings.

Mortgage Pre-Approval Process

Pre-approval strengthens your offer and clarifies your budget before house hunting. Lenders verify income, assets, and credit to determine exact loan amount and terms. This crucial step separates serious buyers from browsers in competitive markets.

Closing Cost Planning

Budget 2-5% of purchase price for closing costs beyond your down payment. These include appraisal, title insurance, origination fees, and prepaid taxes and insurance. Seller concessions and lender credits can reduce your out-of-pocket expenses significantly.

First-Time Homebuyer Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about buying your first home and mortgage options

Minimum credit scores vary by loan type: FHA accepts 580+ for 3.5% down (500-579 with 10% down), conventional requires 620+ for most programs, VA has no official minimum but lenders typically want 620+, and USDA requires 640+ for streamlined approval. Your middle credit score determines qualification, so checking your profile at MiddleCreditScore.com helps you understand which programs fit your situation and what improvements unlock better options.

First-time buyers have multiple low-down-payment options: FHA loans require 3.5% down with 580+ credit, conventional 97% LTV programs need just 3% down for qualified first-timers, VA loans offer 0% down for eligible veterans, and USDA loans provide 0% down in eligible rural areas. Down payment assistance programs can further reduce your out-of-pocket costs by providing grants or forgivable loans to stack with these programs.

The choice depends on your credit score, down payment, and how long you plan to keep the loan. FHA works best for 580-640 credit scores, minimal down payments, or need for maximum flexibility. Conventional is often better with 660+ credit, 5%+ down payment, or planning to keep the loan 10+ years due to PMI removal at 20% equity. FHA mortgage insurance stays for loan life with less than 10% down, while conventional PMI drops off once you reach 20% equity—potentially saving tens of thousands long-term.

The typical timeline is 3-6 months: First, check your credit score and improve if needed (0-3 months). Next, save for down payment and closing costs while researching loan options (ongoing). Then get pre-approved with lenders to understand your budget (1-2 weeks). Start house hunting with your realtor within pre-approved price range (1-3 months). Make an offer and negotiate terms once you find the right property (1-2 weeks). Complete home inspection, appraisal, and final underwriting (3-4 weeks). Finally, close on your home and move in. Working with first-time buyer specialists at Browse Lenders accelerates this process by matching you with experienced professionals who understand new buyer challenges.
