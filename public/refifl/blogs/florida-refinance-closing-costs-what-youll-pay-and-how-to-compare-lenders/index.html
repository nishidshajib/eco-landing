Florida Refinance Closing Costs: What You'll Pay and How to Compare Lenders

Refinance closing costs can run $3,000–$8,000+ in Florida, depending on your loan size and lender. Here’s what you’re actually paying for—and how to shop lenders without getting ripped off.

What’s Included in Refinance Closing Costs

Closing costs break into three categories: lender fees, third-party fees, and prepaid/escrow items.

Lender Fees (Negotiable)

Origination Fee (0.5–1.5% of loan amount) What the lender charges to process your loan. Some lenders call this “points” or “loan discount fees.” This is where shopping pays off—rates vary wildly.

Application Fee ($0–$500) Some lenders charge upfront. Others waive it or roll it into origination.

Underwriting Fee ($400–$900) Covers the cost of reviewing your file and verifying documentation.

Processing Fee ($300–$700) Administrative costs for preparing documents and coordinating the loan.

Not all lenders charge all these fees. Some bundle them into one origination charge. Compare line-by-line, not just the bottom line.

Third-Party Fees (Less Negotiable)

Appraisal ($400–$700 in Florida) Required to confirm property value. You pay even if the refinance doesn’t close.

Title Search and Insurance ($800–$1,500) Title company verifies ownership and insures against defects. Florida is a title insurance state—this isn’t optional.

Settlement/Closing Fee ($300–$600) What the title company or attorney charges to handle closing.

Recording Fees ($50–$200) County charges to record the new mortgage.

Credit Report ($30–$75) Lender pulls a tri-merge report. You pay for it.

Flood Certification ($15–$25) Determines if your property is in a FEMA flood zone.

Survey (Sometimes Required, $300–$500) Some lenders require a new survey. Others accept your original purchase survey if it’s recent.

Prepaid Items and Escrow (Not Actually Fees)

Prepaid Interest ($200–$800) Interest from closing date to the end of the month. The later in the month you close, the less you pay.

Homeowners Insurance (1 year upfront, $1,200–$3,000+) Lenders require proof of insurance before closing. Coastal Florida = higher premiums.

Property Taxes (2–6 months into escrow, varies) If you escrow taxes, lenders collect 2–6 months upfront to cover future payments.

HOA Transfer Fee ($100–$500, if applicable) Some HOAs charge when ownership transfers (even on refinance).

These aren’t fees—they’re expenses you’d pay anyway. But they hit at closing, so budget for them.

How to Read a Loan Estimate

Within 3 business days of applying, lenders must provide a Loan Estimate—a standardized 3-page form that breaks down costs.

Page 1: Loan Terms and Costs

  • Loan amount, interest rate, monthly payment
  • Estimated total closing costs
  • Estimated cash to close

Page 2: Detailed Cost Breakdown

  • Section A: Origination charges (lender fees)
  • Section B: Services you cannot shop for (title, appraisal if lender chooses)
  • Section C: Services you can shop for (title, if you choose the company)
  • Section E: Taxes and government fees
  • Section F: Prepaids
  • Section G: Initial escrow payment

Page 3: Summary and Comparisons

  • 5-year cost comparison
  • APR (interest rate + fees = true cost)
  • Estimated payoff timeline

Compare Loan Estimates side-by-side from multiple lenders. Focus on Section A (origination charges) and total closing costs in Section J.

What You Can Negotiate (and What You Can’t)

Negotiable:

  • Origination fee / points
  • Application fee
  • Processing fee
  • Some title fees (if you shop for your own title company)

Not Negotiable:

  • Appraisal fee (set by appraiser)
  • Recording fees (set by county)
  • Credit report fee
  • Flood certification
  • Property taxes and insurance (set by government/insurer)

Shop lender fees hard. Third-party fees are mostly fixed, but you can save $1,000–$3,000 by comparing lenders’ origination charges.

3 Ways to Compare Florida Refinance Lenders

1. Request Loan Estimates from 3 Lenders Apply to 3 lenders within 14 days. Credit bureaus count multiple mortgage inquiries as one hard pull if done in a short window.

2. Compare APR, Not Just Rate APR includes fees, so a 6.5% rate with $5,000 in fees might have a higher APR than a 6.625% rate with $2,000 in fees. Lower APR = better deal.

3. Calculate Break-Even Total closing costs ÷ monthly savings = months to break even. If you’re paying $4,500 to save $200/month, break-even is 22.5 months. Selling before that? Don’t refinance.

No-Closing-Cost Refinance: Should You Do It?

Some lenders offer “no-closing-cost” refinance. You’re not actually avoiding fees—you’re either:

1. Rolling Costs Into the Loan Your loan balance increases by the cost of closing fees. You pay interest on those fees for 15–30 years.

2. Paying a Higher Rate Lender covers closing costs but bumps your rate 0.25–0.5%. You pay more interest over the loan life.

When It Makes Sense:

  • You’re selling in 2–3 years (break-even is far out)
  • You don’t have cash for closing costs
  • Rates are dropping and you plan to refinance again soon

When It Doesn’t:

  • You’re staying in the home long-term (higher rate costs more over time)
  • You have cash to cover costs (paying upfront saves money)

Run the math. Sometimes paying $4,000 now saves $15,000 over 10 years.

How to Avoid Junk Fees

Watch for these sneaky charges:

Document Preparation Fee Already covered by origination or processing. Challenge this.

Courier Fee Some lenders charge $50–$100 to send documents. This should be included in processing.

Email or Fax Fee Yes, some lenders charge to email you documents. Ridiculous and negotiable.

Notary Fee (if excessive) $50–$100 is normal. $200+? Push back.

If you see fees that seem redundant, ask the lender to explain or waive them. Many will.

When to Walk Away from a Refinance

Closing costs are too high if:

  • Break-even exceeds your timeline. Selling in 3 years but break-even is 5 years? Pass.
  • Total fees exceed 3–4% of loan amount. $8,000 in costs on a $150,000 loan? Shop harder.
  • Lender won’t provide Loan Estimate. Federal law requires it within 3 days of application. No estimate = red flag.
  • Fees change drastically at closing. Minor adjustments are normal. $2,000 in surprise fees? Walk.

Don’t let sunk costs (appraisal fee) trap you into a bad deal. If the numbers don’t work, cancel and refinance when they do.

5-Step Lender Shopping Checklist

Step 1: Request Loan Estimates Apply to 3 Florida refinance lenders within 14 days.

Step 2: Compare Line-by-Line Focus on Section A (origination) and total closing costs.

Step 3: Calculate APR Lower APR = better deal when comparing similar loan terms.

Step 4: Calculate Break-Even Total costs ÷ monthly savings = months to break even.

Step 5: Negotiate Push back on high origination fees or redundant charges.

Don’t settle for the first offer. Lenders compete for your business—make them earn it.

Ready to compare Florida refinance costs?

Request Loan Estimates from multiple lenders, review all fees line-by-line, and calculate break-even points before committing to a refinance. Education first, closing when the numbers make sense.
