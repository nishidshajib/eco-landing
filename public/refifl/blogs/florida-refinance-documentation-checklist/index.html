Florida Refinance Documentation Checklist: What Lenders Actually Need

Refinance documentation feels overwhelming until you know what lenders actually need. Here’s the full checklist, broken down by category, so you can prepare before you start shopping Florida lenders.

Why Documentation Matters for Refinance

Lenders need to verify three things:

  1. You can afford the new payment (income and employment)
  2. You have reserves for unexpected costs (assets)
  3. The property is worth what you claim (appraisal and property docs)

Even if you’re just doing rate-and-term with no cash-out, lenders still verify everything. Refinance isn’t a rubber stamp—it’s a new loan with new underwriting.

Income Documentation (W-2 Employees)

What You’ll Need:

  • 30 days of pay stubs (most recent, showing year-to-date earnings)
  • 2 years of W-2 forms (from all employers)
  • 2 years of federal tax returns (1040s with all schedules)

Why They Want This: Lenders calculate your gross monthly income and verify stability. Two years of employment history in the same field shows income reliability.

Pro Tip: If you switched jobs recently, get a written employment letter from your new employer stating your position, salary, and start date. Same industry = no problem. Different industry = expect more questions.

Income Documentation (Self-Employed)

What You’ll Need:

  • 2 years of personal tax returns (1040s with all schedules)
  • 2 years of business tax returns (1065, 1120, 1120S depending on entity type)
  • Year-to-date profit-and-loss statement (signed and dated)
  • Year-to-date balance sheet (if business owns significant assets)
  • Business bank statements (3–12 months, depending on lender)
  • Business license (if applicable)

Why They Want This: Lenders calculate self-employed income by averaging two years of net income (after deductions). If your income is declining year-over-year, expect questions.

Pro Tip: Aggressive tax write-offs lower your qualifying income. That $80,000 in gross income becomes $50,000 after deductions—lenders use the $50,000 figure.

Asset Documentation

What You’ll Need:

  • 2 months of bank statements (all pages, all accounts)
  • 2 months of investment statements (401k, IRA, brokerage accounts)
  • Proof of other liquid assets (CDs, money market accounts, stocks)

Why They Want This: Lenders verify you have reserves to cover 2–6 months of mortgage payments after closing. Higher loan amounts or lower credit scores = higher reserve requirements.

Pro Tip: Large deposits (anything over 25% of your monthly income) require explanation. Gift funds need a signed letter from the donor stating it’s a gift, not a loan.

Property Documentation

What You’ll Need:

  • Homeowners insurance policy (declarations page showing coverage amounts)
  • Property tax bill (most recent annual or semi-annual statement)
  • HOA documents (if applicable—fees, contact info, proof of insurance)
  • Current mortgage statement (showing balance, rate, payment history)
  • Appraisal (lender orders this, but you pay for it—$400–$700 in Florida)

Why They Want This: Lenders need to confirm property value, verify you’re current on payments, and calculate escrow requirements for taxes and insurance.

Pro Tip: If your home has flood risk, lenders may require flood insurance. Check FEMA flood maps before refinancing—unexpected flood insurance can kill a deal.

Cash-Out Refinance: Extra Documentation

If you’re pulling cash out, lenders want to know how you’ll use it. Be ready to provide:

Debt Consolidation

  • Payoff statements for all debts you’re consolidating
  • Account numbers and creditor contact info

Home Improvements

  • Contractor estimates or bids (if using cash for renovations)
  • Detailed scope of work explaining the project

Other Uses

  • Written explanation of how funds will be used (education, investment, emergency savings)

Lenders don’t dictate how you use cash-out funds, but they want documentation showing responsible planning.

Credit and Identity Documentation

What You’ll Need:

  • Government-issued ID (driver’s license or passport)
  • Social Security card or proof of SSN
  • Credit report authorization (lender provides this form)

Why They Want This: Identity verification prevents fraud. Credit authorization lets lenders pull your tri-merge report.

Pro Tip: If you’ve had a name change (marriage, divorce), provide documentation showing old and new names.

What Happens If You’re Missing Documents

Lenders won’t move forward until they have everything. Missing docs = delayed closing or denial.

Common Issues:

  • Incomplete tax returns (missing schedules or W-2s)
  • Expired insurance (policy needs to be active through closing)
  • Undocumented large deposits (anything over 25% of monthly income)
  • Gap in employment (lenders want explanation letters)

If you’re missing something, ask your lender what’s acceptable as a substitute. Sometimes pay stubs can replace W-2s, or a CPA letter can verify income.

7-Day Documentation Prep Plan

Day 1–2: Income Docs Gather pay stubs, W-2s, and tax returns. Self-employed? Add business returns and P&L.

Day 3–4: Asset Docs Download 2 months of bank and investment statements. Check for large deposits—get ready to explain them.

Day 5: Property Docs Pull your mortgage statement, insurance declarations, and property tax bill.

Day 6: Credit Check Review your middle credit score and dispute any errors you find.

Day 7: Organize and Review Put everything in one digital folder. Label files clearly (e.g., “2024_W2_Employer_Name”). Double-check you have all pages.

How to Share Documents Securely

Never email sensitive documents without encryption. Use:

  • Lender’s secure portal (most Florida lenders provide this)
  • Encrypted file-sharing services (Google Drive, Dropbox with password protection)
  • Secure upload tools (some lenders use DocuSign or similar platforms)

Avoid texting or emailing Social Security numbers, account numbers, or unredacted documents.

Ready to start your Florida refinance?

Gather your documentation, check your credit, and prepare your file before contacting Florida-licensed refinance lenders. Education first, application when you’re fully prepared.
