Mortgage Broker vs Bank: Which is Better for New Hampshire Borrowers?

New Hampshire borrowers choosing between mortgage brokers and retail banks face a fundamental trade-off: multi-lender access and wholesale pricing versus single-lender convenience and brand familiarity.

How Mortgage Brokers Work

Brokers are intermediaries between borrowers and wholesale lenders:

  1. You apply once with the broker
  2. Broker shops 5–20 lenders for best rate/terms
  3. Broker recommends lender based on pricing and approval odds
  4. Wholesale lender underwrites and funds the loan
  5. Broker earns origination fee (1%–2%) or yield spread premium

Brokers don’t lend their own money—they access wholesale lending networks unavailable to consumers directly.

How Retail Banks Work

Banks are direct lenders using their own capital:

  1. You apply with the bank
  2. Bank underwrites in-house using automated or manual systems
  3. Bank prices based on internal costs plus profit margin
  4. Bank funds the loan from deposits or warehouse lines
  5. Bank may sell loan to Fannie/Freddie after closing

You get one lender’s pricing, one set of underwriting overlays, and one approval decision.

Wholesale vs Retail Pricing: The 0.25% Difference

Wholesale lenders price 0.125%–0.375% below retail to compete for broker volume:

Example Portsmouth $450k conventional loan:

Retail Bank:

  • Rate: 6.500%
  • Bank profit margin: built into rate
  • Origination fee: $1,500
  • APR: 6.621%

Mortgage Broker:

  • Rate: 6.250% (wholesale pricing)
  • Broker fee: 1.5% = $6,750
  • APR: 6.548%

Despite visible broker fee, APR is 0.073% lower due to wholesale rate advantage. On $450k, this saves $275/year in interest.

When Brokers Save Money

Brokers typically beat retail banks on:

1. FHA/VA Loans

Wholesale FHA/VA pricing runs 0.125%–0.25% below retail banks because wholesale aggregators (United Wholesale Mortgage, Caliber) specialize in government loan volume.

2. Jumbo Loans

Brokers access 10+ jumbo lenders (private portfolio banks) vs 1–2 at retail banks. Competition drives better pricing on NH jumbo loans >$806,500.

3. Non-QM/Portfolio Loans

Self-employed, investor, or credit-challenged borrowers need portfolio underwriting. Brokers access 5–8 specialty lenders vs most banks having zero portfolio options.

4. Investment Properties

Retail banks often cap investors at 4 properties. Brokers access lenders approving 5–10 financed properties with competitive pricing.

When Banks Save Money

Retail banks sometimes beat brokers on:

1. High-Credit Conventional Loans

NH credit unions (Service CU, Granite State CU) offer competitive retail pricing for 740+ credit scores with standard W-2 income. May beat broker APR by 0.10%–0.15%.

2. Portfolio Relationship Discounts

If you have $100k+ deposits at TD Bank or Citizens, relationship pricing can beat broker wholesale rates by 0.125%–0.25%.

3. Small Loan Amounts

On loans <$200k, broker percentage fees ($2k–$4k) may exceed APR savings from wholesale pricing. Banks with flat $1,000–$1,500 fees can win.

4. Simple Scenarios

Standard purchase, strong credit, W-2 income, 20% down—retail banks price competitively and offer one-stop convenience.

Approval Odds: Broker Multi-Lender Advantage

This is where brokers shine for non-perfect scenarios:

Example: Self-Employed Manchester Buyer

Retail Bank Path:

  • Apply to TD Bank
  • Automated underwriting declines (tax returns show $60k income but business expenses reduce AGI)
  • Start over with Bank of America
  • Same result
  • Give up or pay 20%+ down to force manual underwriting

Broker Path:

  • Apply once with broker
  • Broker submits to portfolio lender using bank statement income (12 months deposits = $95k annual income)
  • Approved at 10% down with competitive portfolio rate
  • One application, one approval

Brokers increase approval odds by matching your profile to the right lender’s underwriting appetite.

Underwriting Overlay Differences

Retail banks often have overlays (rules stricter than Fannie/Freddie minimums):

Common Bank Overlays:

  • Minimum 680 credit (Fannie allows 620)
  • No recent bankruptcy within 4 years (Fannie allows 2–4 years)
  • Maximum 4 financed properties (Fannie allows 10)
  • No cash-out refinance over 70% LTV (Fannie allows 80%)

Brokers access overlay-free wholesale lenders that follow Fannie/Freddie minimums without extra restrictions.

New Hampshire Regional Broker Access

Broker advantages vary by NH market:

Portsmouth/Manchester:

  • 8–12 active brokers compete with 10+ retail banks
  • Wholesale pricing advantage exists but banks stay competitive
  • Broker value: portfolio access for non-QM scenarios

Lakes Region/Northern NH:

  • Fewer retail banks (3–5 active)
  • Brokers provide critical wholesale access to match Portsmouth pricing
  • Seasonal/second home expertise matters—brokers know which lenders approve

Fee Transparency: Broker vs Bank

Broker fees are disclosed in Loan Estimate Section A:

  • Origination charge: 1%–2% of loan amount
  • Or yield spread premium: if you take higher rate, lender pays broker (disclosed)

Bank fees are less visible:

  • Retail margin built into rate (not separately disclosed)
  • Processing, underwriting, application fees (vary by bank)
  • Total APR shows true cost, but harder to see bank profit margin

Service Differences

Brokers:

  • One point of contact (broker) through closing
  • Broker has incentive to close (earns fee at funding)
  • Underwriting happens at wholesale lender (less broker control)
  • May feel more personal/responsive

Banks:

  • Loan officer, processor, underwriter all in-house
  • Bank has less urgency (salaried employees vs commission broker)
  • More control over timeline and conditions
  • Brand familiarity, but may feel more corporate

Processing Speed: Broker vs Bank

Common perception: Banks close faster because everything is in-house.

Reality: Brokers often close as fast or faster:

  • Wholesale lenders (UWM, Caliber) have 7–10 day turn times
  • Brokers pre-package files to reduce underwriting back-and-forth
  • Retail banks sometimes have backlogs during high-volume periods

Typical NH closing timeline: 25–35 days for both brokers and banks on conventional/FHA purchases.

How to Choose: Broker vs Bank in New Hampshire

Use a Broker if:

  • Self-employed, investor, or non-W-2 income
  • Credit score 620–680 (need overlay-free lender)
  • Cash-out refinance over 70%–80% LTV
  • Buying 5th+ financed property
  • Want multi-lender competition for best pricing
  • Previous bank denial (broker can find alternative lender)

Use a Retail Bank if:

  • Strong credit (740+), W-2 income, standard scenario
  • Existing bank relationship with deposit discounts
  • Small loan amount (<$200k) where broker % fees are high
  • Prefer brand familiarity and one-stop banking

Best Strategy: Get Both Quotes

Smart NH borrowers get quotes from:

  1. Local mortgage broker (wholesale multi-lender access)
  2. NH credit union (Service CU, Granite State CU)
  3. National bank (Rocket, Better) for benchmark

Compare APR, not just rate. Factor in broker fee vs retail margin. Choose based on total cost and approval confidence, not just brand name.

NH Broker Licensing Verification

Verify your broker at nmlsconsumeraccess.org:

  • NMLS license number
  • Years in business
  • Any regulatory actions
  • License status in New Hampshire

Licensed NH brokers carry E&O insurance and follow state disclosure requirements.

The Verdict for New Hampshire Borrowers

Mortgage brokers typically win on:

  • Wholesale pricing (0.125%–0.375% rate advantage)
  • Multi-lender access (better approval odds)
  • Portfolio/non-QM loan options
  • Jumbo and investment property financing

Retail banks sometimes win on:

  • High-credit conventional loans at credit unions
  • Relationship deposit discounts
  • Small loan amounts (<$200k)
  • Brand trust and one-stop convenience

For most New Hampshire borrowers—especially self-employed, investors, or those needing portfolio underwriting—brokers provide better pricing and higher approval odds despite visible origination fees. The wholesale rate advantage and lender competition usually outweigh the broker fee when comparing total APR.
