California Loan Programs Every Loan Officer Should Know (CalHFA, Dream For All, Prop 13)

Why California Loan Programs Matter for Loan Officers

California has the most complex mortgage landscape in the United States:

  • Highest median home price ($800K statewide, $1.5M+ in Bay Area/coastal cities)
  • Most down payment assistance programs (50+ state/county/city programs)
  • Unique property tax rules (Prop 13, Prop 19, Prop 60/90)
  • Highest conforming loan limits ($1,149,825 in most counties)
  • Strictest environmental/disclosure laws (earthquake zones, fire zones, coastal commission)

Loan officers who don’t understand California-specific programs leave massive money on the table for borrowers—and lose deals to competitors who do.

This guide covers every major California program loan officers must know to serve first-time buyers, move-up buyers, retirees, and high-income earners across the state.

1. CalHFA Down Payment Assistance Programs

CalHFA (California Housing Finance Agency) is the state agency that administers down payment assistance (DPA) and affordable first mortgages for low-to-moderate income California buyers.

CalHFA MyHome Assistance Program (Most Popular)

What it is: Deferred-payment junior loan (silent second mortgage) for 3%–3.5% of purchase price to cover down payment and/or closing costs.

Key details:

  • Loan amount: 3% (conventional) or 3.5% (FHA) of purchase price
  • Interest rate: 0% (no monthly payment)
  • Repayment: Deferred until you sell, refi, or pay off first mortgage (then CalHFA gets repaid in full)
  • No monthly payment: Silent second—you only pay first mortgage P&I
  • Forgiveness: None (must repay full amount when loan is satisfied)

Income limits (varies by county):

  • Low-income counties (Fresno, Bakersfield, Inland Empire): $150K–180K household income
  • High-cost counties (LA, SF, SD, Orange, Santa Clara): $200K–230K household income

Purchase price limits:

  • Tied to FHA loan limits (varies by county)
  • Most counties: $806,500 (standard conforming limit)
  • High-cost counties: $1,149,825 (SF, LA, Orange, San Diego, Santa Clara, Marin, San Mateo)

Credit score: 660+ (conventional), 640+ (FHA)

Down payment:

  • Conventional: 3% down (CalHFA provides 3%, borrower brings 0%)
  • FHA: 3.5% down (CalHFA provides 3.5%, borrower brings 0%)

First-time buyer requirement: Yes (must not have owned home in past 3 years, except military veterans)

Example scenario:

  • Purchase price: $700K (Los Angeles)
  • Loan program: Conventional 97% LTV + CalHFA MyHome
  • CalHFA assistance: 3% of $700K = $21,000 (silent second)
  • Borrower down payment: $0 (CalHFA covers full 3%)
  • First mortgage: $679,000 (97% LTV)
  • Monthly payment: $4,760 (first mortgage only, no CalHFA payment)
  • Repayment: When borrower sells/refis, pays back $21K to CalHFA (no interest)

Loan officer value: Borrowers who have $10K–15K saved (not enough for 3% down + closing costs) can buy with zero down using CalHFA. Closing costs can be seller-paid or lender credits.

CalHFA Zero Interest Program (ZIP)

What it is: Deferred-payment junior loan for up to $15,000 (or 3% of sales price, whichever is less) for down payment/closing costs.

Key differences from MyHome:

  • Fixed dollar cap: $15K max (vs. MyHome 3% of price)
  • Best for lower-priced homes: If 3% of price is >$15K, use MyHome. If 3% is <$15K, ZIP and MyHome are identical.

Example:

  • $400K purchase: 3% = $12K → ZIP and MyHome both provide $12K
  • $800K purchase: 3% = $24K → MyHome provides $24K, ZIP caps at $15K → Use MyHome

Most loan officers ignore ZIP because MyHome is always equal or better.

CalHFA Extra Credit Teacher Home Purchase Program (ECTP)

What it is: Enhanced DPA for California public school teachers, administrators, and staff.

Assistance amount: 4% of purchase price (vs. 3% for standard buyers)

Who qualifies:

  • Employed by California public school district (K–12)
  • Teachers, administrators, counselors, librarians, staff
  • Must work full-time (part-time teachers don’t qualify)

Income/price limits: Same as MyHome

Example:

  • $650K purchase, teacher buyer
  • CalHFA ECTP: 4% of $650K = $26,000 (silent second)
  • Borrower down payment: $0
  • First mortgage: $624,000 (96% LTV)

Loan officer value: If you work with teachers (huge market in California—300K+ public school employees), always mention ECTP (extra $6,500–10,000 vs. standard MyHome).

CalHFA Forgivable Equity Builder Loan (FEBL)

What it is: Forgivable junior loan that disappears after 5 years if you stay in the home.

Assistance amount: 10% of purchase price (up to $150,000)

Forgiveness: 20%/year over 5 years

  • Year 1: Owe 100%
  • Year 2: Owe 80%
  • Year 3: Owe 60%
  • Year 4: Owe 40%
  • Year 5: Owe 20%
  • Year 6+: $0 owed (fully forgiven)

Interest rate: 0% (no monthly payment)

Who qualifies:

  • First-time buyers (no home ownership in 3 years)
  • Income limits: Lower than MyHome (typically $100K–130K household income, varies by county)
  • Purchase price limits: $500K–650K (varies by county)

Credit score: 660+

Loan officer value: Best for low-income first-time buyers who plan to stay 5+ years (forgiveness = free money). If client might move in 3 years, MyHome is better (FEBL requires partial repayment if you sell early).

Example:

  • $550K purchase, first-time buyer, $110K household income
  • CalHFA FEBL: 10% of $550K = $55,000 (silent second)
  • First mortgage: $495,000 (90% LTV)
  • Borrower stays 5 years → FEBL fully forgiven (free $55K down payment)
  • Borrower stays 3 years, sells → owes 60% of $55K = $33K repayment (still got $22K free)

2. California Dream For All Shared Appreciation Loan

Dream For All is California’s newest down payment assistance program (launched 2023)—offers 20% down payment assistance in exchange for 20% equity share when you sell.

How Dream For All Works

Assistance amount: 20% of purchase price (silent second mortgage)

Interest rate: 0% (no monthly payment)

Repayment: When you sell or refinance, you repay:

  • 20% of original loan amount ($100K if you borrowed $100K)
  • + 20% of home appreciation (if home appreciated)

Example 1: Home appreciates

  • Purchase price: $600K
  • Dream For All assistance: 20% of $600K = $120K
  • Borrower down payment: $0 (or 0.5%–1% for closing costs)
  • First mortgage: $480K (80% LTV)
  • Home sells after 5 years for $750K (25% appreciation)
  • Dream For All repayment: $120K (original loan) + $30K (20% of $150K appreciation) = $150K
  • Net profit: $750K sale price – $480K first mortgage payoff – $150K Dream For All repayment = $120K profit (borrower keeps 80% of appreciation)

Example 2: Home depreciates

  • Purchase price: $600K
  • Dream For All assistance: $120K
  • Home sells after 3 years for $550K (8.3% depreciation)
  • Dream For All repayment: $120K (original loan) – $10K (20% of $50K loss) = $110K
  • Dream For All absorbs 20% of loss—borrower only repays $110K (not full $120K)

Who qualifies:

  • First-time buyers (no home ownership in 3 years)
  • Income limits: $250K household income (higher than CalHFA)
  • Purchase price limits: Up to $1,000,000 (statewide)
  • Credit score: 680+

Loan officer value:

  • Best for high-income first-time buyers (who earn $200K+ but have low savings due to student loans, stock comp vesting schedules, or Bay Area rent)
  • Not ideal for low-appreciation markets (if home appreciates 2%/year, borrower shares 20% of minimal gains)
  • Risk: If home appreciates 50%, borrower gives up 20% of equity (which could be $100K+ in Bay Area)

Example: Tech worker in San Jose

  • Household income: $220K (high for CalHFA, but qualifies for Dream For All)
  • Purchase price: $950K
  • Dream For All: 20% of $950K = $190K down payment
  • First mortgage: $760K
  • Borrower brings: $0 down (closing costs covered by lender credits or seller)
  • 5 years later, home worth $1.3M (37% appreciation)
  • Dream For All repayment: $190K + 20% of $350K appreciation = $190K + $70K = $260K
  • Borrower keeps: $1.3M – $760K (mortgage payoff) – $260K (Dream For All) = $280K profit

Dream For All vs. CalHFA:

  • Dream For All: Higher assistance (20% vs. 3%), higher income limits ($250K vs. $200K), but must share appreciation
  • CalHFA MyHome: Lower assistance (3%), lower income limits, but no appreciation sharing (only repay principal)

3. Proposition 13 and Property Tax Portability (Prop 19)

Proposition 13 (passed 1978) is California’s constitutional limit on property tax increases:

  • Initial assessed value = purchase price
  • Annual increase cap = 2%/year max (even if market value rises 10%/year)
  • Reassessment only when property sells (new owner pays tax on new purchase price)

Why this matters for loan officers: California homeowners who bought 10–30 years ago have massive property tax savings (paying taxes on $300K assessed value even though home is worth $1.5M today). They fear moving because new purchase triggers reassessment → 3x–5x higher property tax bill.

Proposition 19 (Tax Portability for Seniors 55+)

Prop 19 (passed 2020, effective 2021) allows homeowners age 55+, disabled, or wildfire/disaster victims to transfer their old property tax base to a new home anywhere in California (previously limited to same county or 10 specific counties).

How Prop 19 works:

Old rule (Prop 60/90, pre-2021):

  • Homeowner age 55+ could transfer tax base once in lifetime
  • Only within same county (or to 10 approved counties: LA, Orange, San Diego, Ventura, Santa Clara, San Mateo, Alameda, El Dorado, Riverside, San Bernardino)
  • New home must be equal or lesser value than old home

New rule (Prop 19, 2021+):

  • Homeowner age 55+ can transfer tax base up to 3 times in lifetime
  • Transfer to any county in California (not limited to same county)
  • New home can be any value (if new home is more expensive, assessed value is adjusted upward proportionally)

Example 1: Equal-value purchase

  • Current home: Purchased 1990 for $200K, now worth $1.2M, assessed value $300K (Prop 13 cap)
  • Property tax: $300K × 1.2% = $3,600/year
  • Sells for $1.2M, buys new home for $1.2M (equal value)
  • Prop 19 transfer: New home assessed at $300K (old tax base transfers)
  • New property tax: $3,600/year (same as before)
  • Savings vs. no portability: $1.2M × 1.2% = $14,400/year → saves $10,800/year by transferring tax base

Example 2: Move-up purchase (new home more expensive)

  • Current home: Assessed value $400K, sells for $1M
  • Buys new home for $1.5M (50% more expensive)
  • Prop 19 formula: $400K (old base) + $500K (difference in purchase price) = $900K assessed value
  • New property tax: $900K × 1.2% = $10,800/year
  • Savings vs. no portability: $1.5M × 1.2% = $18,000/year → saves $7,200/year

Loan officer value:

  • Unlock senior move-up buyers who were “stuck” in old homes (feared tax increases). Prop 19 allows them to buy larger/better homes (move closer to grandkids, downsize to single-story, relocate to wine country) without losing tax benefits.
  • Target markets: Retirees moving from Bay Area to Tahoe, Sacramento, San Diego (tax base transfers).
  • Marketing angle: “You can move anywhere in California and keep your low property taxes” (huge selling point for 55+ buyers).

4. High-Balance Conforming Loan Limits (California-Specific)

Standard conforming limit (2024): $766,550 (nationwide)

High-balance conforming limit (2024): $1,149,825 (California high-cost counties)

California counties with $1,149,825 limit (most populous):

  • Los Angeles, Orange, San Diego, Riverside, San Bernardino
  • San Francisco, Santa Clara, San Mateo, Alameda, Contra Costa, Marin
  • Ventura, Sacramento, Monterey, Santa Cruz, Sonoma, Napa

Why this matters: Loans up to $1,149,825 are conforming (Fannie/Freddie) → lower rates, easier underwriting, 3%–5% down options. Above $1,149,825 = jumbo → stricter guidelines, 10%–20% down, higher rates.

Example:

  • $1.1M purchase in San Jose (Santa Clara County)
  • Conforming loan: 5% down ($55K), rate 6.50%, conventional 95% LTV
  • If this were in Fresno (standard $766,550 limit), $1.1M = jumbo → 20% down ($220K), rate 6.875%, much harder to qualify

Loan officer value: Know which counties have high-balance limits. Borrowers buying $800K–1.1M homes in California expect conforming treatment (5% down, competitive rates). If you mistakenly treat as jumbo, you lose the deal to a loan officer who knows better.

5. Regional/Local Down Payment Assistance Programs

California has 50+ county/city DPA programs (beyond CalHFA). Loan officers must know local programs in their markets.

Los Angeles County

LA County Development Authority (LACDA) Home Ownership Program:

  • Assistance: 3% down payment loan (deferred, 0% interest)
  • Income limits: $110K–130K household
  • Purchase price: Up to $650K
  • Credit: 640+

San Francisco

SF Mayor’s Office of Housing and Community Development (MOHCD):

  • Down Payment Assistance Loan Program (DALP): Up to $375,000 (yes, $375K!) for low-income buyers
  • Income limits: $120K–150K household (strict)
  • Purchase price: Up to $1M (condos/townhomes)
  • Repayment: Deferred until sale/refi, shared appreciation (city takes % of equity)

San Diego

San Diego Housing Commission:

  • First-Time Homebuyer Program: Up to $30,000 DPA (silent second, 0% interest)
  • Income limits: $150K household
  • Purchase price: Up to $950K
  • Credit: 680+

Orange County

Orange County Housing Authority:

  • First-Time Homebuyer Program: 3% DPA (deferred, 0% interest)
  • Income limits: $130K–160K household
  • Purchase price: Up to $800K

Inland Empire (Riverside/San Bernardino)

Inland Empire Homeownership Programs:

  • Assistance: $10K–20K DPA (deferred)
  • Income limits: $100K household
  • Purchase price: Up to $500K

Loan officer value: Layer CalHFA (state program) + local DPA = $25K–50K total assistance. Example: LA buyer uses CalHFA $21K + LACDA $20K = $41K down payment (borrower brings $0).

6. California First-Time Buyer Tax Credit (FTHB)

Mortgage Credit Certificate (MCC): California offers federal tax credit (not deduction—actual dollar-for-dollar reduction in taxes owed) for first-time buyers.

How MCC works:

  • Buyer gets 20%–30% of annual mortgage interest back as tax credit
  • Example: Pay $20K mortgage interest/year, 20% MCC = $4,000 tax credit (reduces federal taxes owed by $4K)
  • Credit is annual (every year you have the mortgage)
  • Lifetime value: $4K/year × 10 years = $40K in tax savings

Who qualifies:

  • First-time buyers (no home ownership in 3 years)
  • Income limits: $150K–200K household (varies by county)
  • Purchase price limits: $800K–1.1M (varies by county)

Trade-off: MCC reduces mortgage interest deduction (you can’t double-dip—if you claim $4K MCC credit, you deduct $16K interest instead of $20K). But credit > deduction for most buyers (saves more taxes).

Loan officer value: Every first-time buyer should evaluate MCC. Combine with CalHFA → buyer gets DPA + tax credits (stacks perfectly). Apply through county housing authority (not all counties offer—check local).

7. California Environmental/Disclosure Requirements Loan Officers Must Know

Natural Hazard Disclosure (NHD)

California requires sellers to disclose natural hazard zones:

  • Earthquake fault zones (Alquist-Priolo zones)
  • Flood zones (FEMA maps)
  • Fire hazard zones (Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zones—VHFHSZ)
  • Tsunami/seiche zones (coastal areas)

Loan officer impact: Lenders require hazard insurance (earthquake, flood, fire) if property is in high-risk zone. Some lenders won’t lend in extreme fire zones (Paradise, areas burned in 2017–2023 fires).

What to do: Check NHD report early (order with appraisal). If property is in fire zone, confirm lender will accept + verify insurance availability (some insurers pulled out of California fire zones).

Coastal Commission Approval

Properties within California Coastal Zone (within ~1 mile of coast) require Coastal Development Permits for major renovations. Lenders may require Coastal Commission approval for condition/title clearance.

Loan officer impact: Delays closings (Coastal Commission reviews take 30–90 days). If buyer plans renovation, flag this upfront.

8. California Self-Employed/1099 Loan Programs

California has highest concentration of self-employed workers in U.S. (gig economy, tech contractors, real estate agents, freelancers). Standard Fannie/Freddie loans require 2 years tax returns, which often understate income (write-offs, depreciation).

California-specific solutions:

Bank Statement Loans

Qualify based on 12–24 months business/personal bank deposits (instead of tax returns).

Example:

  • Borrower: Freelance graphic designer
  • Tax returns show $80K income (after write-offs)
  • Bank statements show $150K deposits/year (gross revenue)
  • Bank statement lender qualifies at $150K income (or 50%–75% of deposits, depending on lender)

Rates: 6.75%–7.50% (0.25%–0.75% higher than conventional)

Down payment: 10%–15% min (vs. 3% conventional)

Lenders: Angel Oak, Deephaven, Acra, Verus (brokers access these; banks typically don’t)

1099 Income Loans

Qualify using 1099 forms (instead of full tax returns).

Example:

  • Tech contractor, $180K on 1099s
  • Tax returns show $120K (after home office, mileage, equipment write-offs)
  • 1099 lender qualifies at $180K gross income

Loan officer value: California self-employed buyers expect you to know non-QM options. If you say “sorry, your tax returns don’t show enough income,” they’ll find a broker who does bank statement loans.

9. Stock Compensation Underwriting (Bay Area/LA Tech Hubs)

California tech workers receive 30%–60% of comp as stock (RSUs, options, ESPP). Fannie/Freddie allow stock comp as income, but underwriting varies wildly by lender.

Stock comp types:

RSUs (Restricted Stock Units)

Vested RSUs: Count as income (most lenders require 2 years history, average vested amounts)

Unvested RSUs: Some lenders count 50% of unvested (if vesting within 12 months). Others count 0%.

Example:

  • Base salary: $150K
  • RSUs vesting in 2024: $100K
  • Some lenders qualify at $150K + $100K = $250K
  • Others qualify at $150K only (don’t count unvested)

Stock Options (ISOs/NSOs)

Most lenders don’t count until exercised and sold (too speculative). Exception: Post-IPO companies with liquid stock (e.g., Google, Meta, Apple employees—lenders count as regular income).

ESPP (Employee Stock Purchase Plan)

If borrower has 2 years history of ESPP participation + selling shares, lenders count as bonus income (average sales over 2 years).

Loan officer value: Bay Area/LA loan officers MUST understand stock comp. If you say “we can’t count your RSUs,” borrower finds another loan officer who can. Partner with jumbo lenders who specialize in tech workers (First Republic—RIP—used to dominate this; now UWM, Pennymac, and portfolio lenders fill gap).

10. California Refinance Programs (Cash-Out Restrictions)

Prop 58/193 Parent-Child Transfer Exclusion (Eliminated 2021)

Old rule (pre-2021): Parents could transfer primary residence to children without property tax reassessment (child inherited parent’s low Prop 13 tax base).

New rule (Prop 19, 2021+): Parent-child transfer triggers reassessment UNLESS:

  • Child uses home as primary residence (must move in within 1 year)
  • Home value <$1M above assessed value (if parent’s assessed value is $300K and home worth $1.3M, no reassessment; if worth $1.5M, reassessed on $200K excess)

Loan officer impact: Fewer parent-child transfers (tax penalty). If child wants to keep home, must qualify for mortgage (can’t assume parent’s loan if child doesn’t live there). Plan for refinance if child inherits.

California Cash-Out Refi (Fannie/Freddie)

California is “non-recourse” state for purchase loans (lender can’t pursue borrower personally if foreclosed—can only take property). But cash-out refis are recourse (lender can sue for deficiency if underwater).

Loan officer value: Explain to borrowers that cash-out refi = recourse loan (you lose non-recourse protection from original purchase). Some borrowers prefer HELOC instead (keeps first mortgage non-recourse, second mortgage is recourse but smaller loan).

Red Flags: When Loan Officers Lose California Deals

❌ Don’t know CalHFA exists: Borrower has $15K saved, needs $21K down. You say “sorry, not enough.” Competitor mentions CalHFA, closes deal.

❌ Don’t know Prop 19: 60-year-old client says “I can’t move, my property taxes will triple.” You agree. Competitor explains Prop 19, shows $7K/year savings, closes $1.5M deal.

❌ Don’t know high-balance conforming limits: Borrower buying $1.1M home in LA. You quote jumbo (20% down, 6.875%). Competitor quotes conforming (5% down, 6.50%). You lose.

❌ Don’t know bank statement loans: Self-employed borrower, tax returns show $90K. You decline. Competitor does bank statement loan (qualifies at $140K), closes deal.

❌ Don’t know stock comp underwriting: Tech worker, $200K salary + $150K RSUs. You qualify at $200K only (debt ratios don’t work). Competitor counts RSUs, qualifies at $350K, closes $1.8M jumbo.

Bottom Line: California Loan Officers Must Specialize

California is not a generic mortgage market. Loan officers who treat it like Texas, Florida, or Arizona lose deals daily to California specialists who know:

✅ CalHFA (3% DPA, ECTP for teachers, FEBL forgiveness)
✅ Dream For All (20% DPA, appreciation sharing)
✅ Prop 19 (tax portability for 55+)
✅ High-balance conforming ($1,149,825 limits in most counties)
✅ Local DPA (LA, SF, SD, OC, IE programs)
✅ Bank statement loans (self-employed, 1099, gig workers)
✅ Stock comp underwriting (RSUs, options, ESPP)
✅ Natural hazard zones (fire, flood, earthquake insurance)
